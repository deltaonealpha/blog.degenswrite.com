Header Title Design - Inside Look

Tomato Cultivator | @deltaonealpha

DEGENSWRITE CORPORATION

A brief look into our new design for title headers and how we came to the final iteration, after hundreds of trials and polls.

As we progress further in preparing degenswrite for its launch, we are working on perfecting, polishing and further improving the UI every single day.

The title-page header. A single block to name, describe and visualize the whole piece of literature below it. This header is what creates the difference between a reader choosing to read the title or to not. The design, matters.

In pursuit of perfecting this we've been through tens and twenties of variations. Starting with the original full-width implementation to the current tile-like "floating" design.

The floating design gives an illusion of depth, creating a whole new dimension of immersive experience.

The header tile is made with 6 distinct layers.
  Layer 1 - the image/ GIF of the title
  Layer 2 - darkening layer (darkens image to black by a certain degree)
  Layer 3 - linear horizontal blur layer
  Layer 4 - gaussian blur layer
  Layer 5 - heading + description + black bars for other metrics
  Layer 6 - other metrics (views/ estimated reading time)

For this 6-layer design schema, we tried 20 different combinations of various blur intensities and types, no blur at all and even different degrees of darkening, with polls, in-depth analysis of visibility across numerous samples and finally settled on the current design.

These are some iterations we went through:

PROTOTYPE - 0 BLUR 0 DARKEN
No distinction between layers, illegible text.

PROTOTYPE - 4 BLUR 0 DARKEN
Blur reduces details, text still illegible.

PROTOTYPE - 0 BLUR 0.7 DARKEN
Lighter parts of background merge with text at spots.

PROTOTYPE - 4 BLUR 0.7 DARKEN
All details on the background lost.

FINAL SELECTION - 4 BLUR 0.7 DARKEN

With a 1.2-pixel wide blur profile and 0.8% darkening to black, we have stuck the perfect balance between visibility, details and creating a distinct separated between elements of each layer.
